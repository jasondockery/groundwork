The daily path

Groundwork Twelve.

84 days from zero to AI-native development habits. Twelve weeks, one focused hour on weekdays, one deep build every Saturday — with real progress you can point at, and an agent that starts as your tutor and ends as a delegate you supervise.

Groundwork Twelve combines pieces that are usually separate: from-zero tool fluency, a day-by-day schedule, hours-deep practice that builds muscle memory, honest progress measurement, and directing AI agents as a first-class skill. It sequences the pages already in this guide — each day tells you what to do, how long it takes, and where to read.

It's written in layers, like everything here. If you already know a week's material, take its skill check on Monday: pass it and skip ahead, and let the plan slow you down only where your fingers (not your understanding) need the reps.

#The weekly rhythm

Monday–FridayOne focused hour: roughly 20 minutes of drills (repeating earlier skills), 25 minutes of new material, 10 minutes of practice, 5 minutes writing your log line and committing it.
SaturdayThe deep build: 2–4 hours, blank canvas, using only that week's skills. This is not extra practice — it is the week's assessment. Every Saturday produces an artifact that goes in your repo.
SundayRest, or catch up on a missed day. A sustainable pace beats a heroic one.

Life happens, so every day has three modes. The streak survives as long as you do the minimum:

Full day60–75 minutes — the plan as written.
Minimum day15 minutes — open the terminal, run one five-minute drill from earlier in the week, write one log line, commit it. That's a real day.
Catch-up day90–120 minutes — yesterday's material plus today's. Never stack more than one missed day; if you're two behind, skip the older one and keep moving.
Recruit one buddy

People finish paced programs together far more often than alone. One friend on the same schedule — or even one person who just reads your log once a week — changes the odds more than any tool on this site.

#How progress is measured

Four layers, no gimmicks. No points, no badges, no timers — artifacts and observable skills.

1. The daily logYour log is a Git repository — creating it is Day 1. One line per day, committed daily. The streak is git log, so measuring your progress is itself Git practice. Push it to GitHub for public accountability if that motivates you.
2. Weekly skill checksEvery week ends with a short "can you do this without the cheat sheet?" list. Observable, not timed. If you can't, repeat the drills — that's information, not failure.
3. The agent as examinerOnce a week, ask your agent to quiz you (prompt below). It reads your log, sees what you've been avoiding, and gives you real feedback — the thing self-paced learning usually lacks.
4. Saturday artifactsTwelve builds, one per week, all in your repo — ending with a capstone. By Day 84 your repo is a portfolio of proof, not a certificate.

The log template

Day 1 creates ~/groundwork-log. Start log.md with this and add one line each day:

log.md
# Groundwork Twelve — my log

Day 1 (2026-07-07): Created this repo. pwd/ls/cd feel okay; rm scares me. 60m
Day 2 (2026-07-08): Navigation drill x5. Stopped reaching for the mouse twice. 60m
Day 3 (2026-07-09): Minimum day — one drill, this line. 15m

Each Sunday, add a short weekly review under a heading: what got easier, what still feels slow, and one thing to repeat next week. Honest beats impressive — the log is for you.

The examiner prompt

Once a week (each Friday from Week 4 on), start your agent in the log repo and paste this:

agent examiner
Read log.md and this week's files. You are my examiner, not my helper.
Give me five small tasks that test this week's skills, one at a time,
and wait for me to do each in my terminal and report back.
Don't show me how. After the five, tell me: what am I avoiding,
what should I repeat next week, and one thing I did well.

#Agent rules by phase

The agent is present from Day 1 — but what it's allowed to do grows as your fundamentals harden. This ladder is the heart of the plan: an agent that does the work for you in Week 2 quietly robs you of the practice that makes Week 10 possible. Put these rules in your prompt when you start a session.

Weeks 1–3
Explainer		Allowed: explain commands and concepts, quiz you, point out mistakes after you try, generate practice drills.
Not allowed: write commands for you to copy blindly, edit files for you, solve a drill before you've attempted it.
Weeks 4–6
Reviewer		Allowed: everything above, plus review your commits and diffs, critique your scripts, give you a recovery plan when you're stuck.
Not allowed: make the commits, fix the script itself, or run recovery commands you don't understand yet.
Weeks 7–9
Bounded pair		Allowed: everything above, plus implement small pieces after you've written the spec or failing test, and suggest edge cases you missed.
Not allowed: choose what to build, or merge anything you haven't read line by line.
Weeks 10–12
Delegate		Allowed: own bounded tasks end to end — plan, edit, run checks, report back with a diff and verification notes.
Not allowed: skip the plan, skip verification, or commit. You own the merge — that never changes.
Why restrict a tool you're here to learn?

Because judgment is the skill. An agent can produce working output on Day 1, but the ability to direct, review, and verify that output only grows out of hands-on fluency. You climb the ladder as fast as your checks pass — the restriction is scaffolding, not punishment.

#The arc

Weeks 1–3Machine fluency: keyboard, terminal, files, the modern shell tools, tmux. Agent explains and quizzes only.
Weeks 4–6Craft: Git for real, your editor, pipes and scripts, publishing to GitHub. Agent becomes your reviewer.
Weeks 7–9Programming and narrow agent direction: write code by hand, then learn to hand the agent small, checkable targets. Agent becomes a bounded pair.
Weeks 10–12Repo-scale direction: specs, plans, verification, judgment. Agent becomes a delegate you supervise. Capstone.

#Week 1 — Keyboard, terminal, and your log

Read Getting started alongside this week. Everything happens in the terminal from the first minute.

1

Day 1 · Create your learning repo (60m)

Open Ghostty. Learn pwd, ls, cd, mkdir, touch. Then the ritual — you'll understand it fully in Week 4; for now, trust it: mkdir ~/groundwork-log && cd ~/groundwork-log && git init, create log.md with the template above, then git add -A && git commit -m "day 1". Ask the agent to explain each command — not to run them.

2

Day 2 · Move without the mouse (60m)

Terminal navigation keys: Tab completion, CtrlA/E, AltB/F, CtrlL. Run the walk-the-tree drill five times, faster each round without rushing. Log what felt slow.

3

Day 3 · Files and folders (60m)

Create, move, copy, rename, and delete files in a scratch folder under /tmp — mv, cp, rm -i. Make one deliberate mistake (delete the wrong file) and recover by recreating it; feel why Week 4 exists. Commit your log.

4

Day 4 · Shell comfort (60m)

History with CtrlR, --help and man, the survival kit (CtrlC, q, CtrlD). Re-run yesterday's file drill using only recalled history — type nothing you've typed before.

5

Day 5 · tmux, first contact (60m)

tmux new -s twelve. Split panes, move between them with Alth/j/k/l, detach with Ctrla then d, reattach. Then take the Week 1 skill check below.

Saturday build · Day 6 (2–3h)

Your workspace, repeatable. From a fresh terminal, build the setup you'll learn in every day from now on: a tmux session with a pane for work, a pane for notes, your log open. Then write workspace.md in your repo — the exact steps to open it from cold — and commit it. Blank canvas: no copying from this page, only from your own history and notes.

Week 1 check — without the cheat sheet: navigate to any folder and back; create, move, and delete a file; recall a command with CtrlR; start, split, detach, and reattach tmux; seven commits in your log.

#Week 2 — The daily drivers

The modern tools that make the terminal fast: Shell & CLI is this week's reading. Clone a real project to explore: git clone https://github.com/jasondockery/groundwork ~/code/groundwork-explore.

Day 8 · MonListing and reading: ll, lt (eza), bat. Explore the cloned repo's structure without opening a file manager.
Day 9 · TueSearch inside files: rg, smart case, rg -t, then rgf for fuzzy search. Find every mention of "tmux" in the cloned repo.
Day 10 · WedFind files and jump folders: fd, then z (zoxide). Visit three folders, jump between them by fragment.
Day 11 · ThuFuzzy everything: CtrlT, AltC. Chain the loop: find a file, search inside it, read it — keyboard only, five times.
Day 12 · FriHistory mastery: Atuin's CtrlR across sessions. Build cheatsheet.md in your repo: your personal top ten commands, pulled from your real history. Skill check.
Saturday build · Day 13 (2–3h)

Scavenger hunt. Ask your agent to write ten questions about the cloned repo ("which file defines the tmux prefix?", "how many HTML pages are in docs/?"). Answer all ten using only terminal tools, and commit hunt.md with each question, the command you used, and the answer. Agent writes questions and grades — it does not answer.

Week 2 check: find any file by name and any string by content in a repo you've never seen; jump between three projects with z; resurrect yesterday's commands without retyping them.

#Week 3 — tmux and the machine

Reading: tmux, plus macOS desktop and Keyboard. On Linux/WSL, swap Day 18 for your window manager's equivalents — the tools are choices; the keyboard-first habit is the point.

Day 15 · MonSessions, windows, panes — the full model. Create, rename, and reorder windows; zoom a pane; resize.
Day 16 · TuePersistence: detach mid-task, close the terminal app entirely, reattach — nothing died. Meet the sesh switcher (Ctrla then T).
Day 17 · WedCopy mode and scrollback: search output you've already scrolled past, copy it without the mouse.
Day 18 · ThuThe desktop layer: app switching, window snaps with Raycast, tabs — the keyboard path for everything outside the terminal.
Day 19 · FriConsolidation: ten minutes of shortcut reps, then write keymap.md — the fifteen keys you now own. Skill check.
Saturday build · Day 20 (2–3h)

Workspace as code. Turn Week 1's workspace.md into something executable: a small script or sesh entry that opens your whole learning workspace — session, panes, log — in one command. Document it, commit it, and use it every day from now on.

Week 3 check: cold start to full workspace in one command; move between editor, terminal, and browser without touching the mouse; survive a detach in the middle of real work.

#Week 4 — Git for real

Reading: Git. The Day 1 ritual becomes understanding. The agent is now allowed to review — end each day asking it to critique your commits.

Day 22 · MonThe core loop: git status, git diff, git add -p, small commits with honest messages. Re-read your Day 1 ritual and explain every word of it in your log.
Day 23 · TueBranches: create, switch, merge. Build a tiny change on a branch and land it.
Day 24 · WedReading history: git log, git show, lg (lazygit) to see it all at once.
Day 25 · ThuUndo without fear: git restore, git revert, when to reach for reset. Break something on purpose; recover it three ways.
Day 26 · FriFirst examiner session: the agent reads your week's commits and quizzes you (prompt above). Skill check.
Saturday build · Day 27 (2–3h)

A history worth reading. Build a small text project — your cheatsheet grown into a real guide, a recipe collection, anything — in eight or more atomic commits with clear messages, including one documented mistake and its recovery. The artifact is the git log itself.

Week 4 check: stage half a file with add -p; explain what HEAD is in one sentence; undo an unstaged change, a staged change, and a bad commit; branch and merge without consulting anything.

#Week 5 — The editor

Reading: Neovim. If you've chosen VS Code or another editor instead, do the same drills with its keyboard equivalents — fluent editing is the requirement; the editor is your choice.

Day 29 · MonSurvival and motion: modes, h j k l, words and lines, :w, :q. Twenty minutes of pure motion drills.
Day 30 · TueEdit verbs: delete, change, yank — ciw, dd, yy, undo. Edit yesterday's log entry ten different ways.
Day 31 · WedSearch and multi-file: /, replace, splits, jumping between open files.
Day 32 · ThuThe LazyVim layer: Space menu, fuzzy file open, file tree — or your editor's command palette equivalents.
Day 33 · FriOne workspace: editor splits and tmux panes as a single navigable grid with Alth/j/k/l. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 34 (2–3h)

Keyboard-only refactor. Reorganize your whole learning repo — split log.md weeks into files, tidy your notes into folders — entirely in the editor, no mouse, committed atomically. The constraint is the exercise.

Week 5 check: make five common edits without arrow keys or mouse; open any file by fuzzy name; move between editor and terminal panes without thinking about it.

#Week 6 — Pipes, scripts, and publishing

Reading: Command line & Unix, and the remote sections of Git.

Day 36 · MonPipes and redirection: |, >, wc, sort, uniq, head — answer real questions about your log with one-liners ("how many minimum days so far?").
Day 37 · TueFirst script: shebang, chmod +x, run it, then shellcheck it and fix what it flags.
Day 38 · WedVariables and loops in a script — harden your Week 3 workspace opener to survive being run twice.
Day 39 · ThuPublish: push your log repo to GitHub with gh, write a real README. Your streak is public now, if you want it to be.
Day 40 · FriPull requests on your own repo: branch, push, gh pr create, read your own diff with delta, merge. Agent reviews the script and the PR. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 41 (2–4h)

A CLI tool of your own. One script that does one thing you actually want — a log stats summary, a project opener, a daily note generator. README, usage examples, shellcheck-clean, landed by PR. This is the first build a stranger could use.

Week 6 check: compose a pipeline from scratch to answer a question; write, run, and lint a script; push a branch and open a PR without looking anything up.

#Week 7 — Programming, by hand

Python via uv — the interpreter is already managed for you (Development). Agent rule change: it may now implement after you've written a spec or failing test. Not before.

Day 43 · MonThe REPL, variables, types. Play — compute things about your log.
Day 44 · TueControl flow: if, loops. Fair warning: this is where most beginner journeys stall. Go slow, drill twice, and log the confusion honestly — it passes.
Day 45 · WedLoops again, plus lists. Repetition is the plan, not a setback.
Day 46 · ThuFunctions: extract yesterday's work into named, reusable pieces.
Day 47 · FriFirst test: pytest, one failing test, make it pass by hand. Red, green — the loop that anchors everything later. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 48 (2–4h)

A program with tests. Small and real: a log analyzer that reports your streak, day modes, and total hours. Written by hand, tests first where you can. When it works, ask the agent for a code review — then improve it yourself based on what you agree with.

#Week 8 — Programs that do things

Day 50 · MonFiles: read and write from Python — your log is your dataset.
Day 51 · TueStructured data: JSON or CSV in and out.
Day 52 · WedFirst bounded delegation: write a spec and a failing test for one tiny function, hand both to the agent, review its diff line by line, run the test. Log how it felt.
Day 53 · ThuMake something visible: a tiny local web page or terminal app around your data — whichever direction pulls you.
Day 54 · FriReading unfamiliar code: pick one file in a real project, trace it with rg, ask the agent to explain what you can't untangle. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 55 (2–4h)

A small app, human-led. Extend Saturday's analyzer into something with a face — a web page, a rich terminal UI, a daily report. You design and lead; the agent implements only pieces you've specified and only after your tests exist.

#Week 9 — Directing agents on narrow work

Reading: Editors & AI — the loop, concrete targets, and knowing your budget. This week the skill under practice is direction.

Day 57 · MonThe loop, deliberately: describe → plan → edit → review diff → verify, on three tiny tasks. Reject one plan on purpose and re-describe.
Day 58 · TueFailing-test-first with the agent: you write the red test, it makes it green, you verify. Twice.
Day 59 · WedVisual targets: give the agent a screenshot or sketch of what you want and say "build this." Compare against prose-only for the same task.
Day 60 · ThuBudget day: /context, /usage, clean sessions, and btop while an agent works — know what everything costs.
Day 61 · FriCritical review: for every agent diff today, name one concrete risk out loud before accepting. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 62 (2–4h)

An agent-assisted feature. Add a real feature to your Week 8 app: you write the spec and the tests, the agent implements, you review and verify everything. Commit a verification.md recording what you checked and how — that document is the artifact.

#Week 10 — Specs, plans, and instructions

Reading: AI agents & rules and Starting a project. Final rule change: the agent may now own bounded tasks end to end. You own the merge.

Day 64 · MonWrite AGENTS.md for your project: how to run it, test it, what never to touch.
Day 65 · TuePlan-first: give the agent a meaty task, demand the plan, and edit the plan before any file changes.
Day 66 · WedMulti-file change against a written checklist — the diff-shape habit at real scale.
Day 67 · ThuStart a second project from scratch with proper scaffolding, instructions included from minute one.
Day 68 · FriTeach the agent your conventions: refine AGENTS.md until a fresh session gets it right first try. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 69 (2–4h)

A delegated feature, verified. Spec a multi-file feature with a checklist, delegate it whole, then verify every checklist item yourself before merging. The spec, the diff, and your verification notes go in the repo together.

#Week 11 — Verification and judgment

Reading: Everyday workflow and the security chop on the agentic engineering page.

Day 71 · MonVerification habits: for each change today, decide what proof "done" requires before starting — then collect exactly that proof.
Day 72 · TueSecurity pass on your own projects: secrets, injection paths, what could leak. Ask the agent to attack its own earlier work.
Day 73 · WedUnfamiliar territory: clone a small open-source project and scope one tiny improvement — a doc fix, a test, a small bug.
Day 74 · ThuRecovery: /rewind, git revert, knowing when to abandon a polluted session and start clean.
Day 75 · FriCapture: start your knowledge base and move the durable lessons out of your log. Skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 76 (2–4h)

The unfamiliar-repo task. Complete Wednesday's scoped improvement end to end in the foreign codebase — agent delegated, everything verified, written up. Working in code you didn't write is the truest test so far.

#Week 12 — Capstone

One small, real thing you want to exist — built the way you now work: planned, directed, verified, explained.

Day 78 · MonChoose and spec it: goals, invariants, what "done" proves. Keep it deliberately small — the craft is the point, not the size.
Day 79–81 · Tue–ThuBuild days: direct the agent, verify as you go, keep the log honest about what it did versus what you did.
Day 82 · FriHarden: tests green, security look, README a stranger could follow. Final skill check + examiner.
Saturday build · Day 83 (2–4h)

Finish and explain. Ship the capstone, then write the explanation that matters: what it does, how you verified it, what the agent did and what you did, and what you'd do differently. That write-up is the proof this program worked.

Day 84 · Sunday: the retrospective. Read your whole log start to finish — Day 1 you and Day 84 you are different people. Then choose what's next.

#After Day 84

You have habits, a portfolio repo, and an agent you know how to direct. Point it at something you love: a week-long game, web, app, or media track — or straight at the longer path toward working like an engineer. The daily log doesn't have to stop; only the schedule does.
